Dipole Vectors & Molecular Polarity
What is a dipole vector?

A dipole is a separation of charge — one end of a bond is slightly negative (δ−) and the other is slightly positive (δ+). We represent it as an arrow (vector): it has both a direction (which way the electrons are pulled) and a magnitude (how strongly — proportional to ΔEN).

δ+ H F δ−
Arrow points toward higher electronegativity (δ−). Longer arrow = bigger ΔEN = stronger dipole.
How vectors cancel — or don't

Dipole arrows behave like forces: two equal arrows pointing in exactly opposite directions cancel out (net = zero). If they point in different directions that don't fully oppose each other, there is a net dipole — the molecule is polar.

CO₂ — nonpolar
H₂O — polar

02 Dipole Vectors & Cancellation ▼
A dipole vector is an arrow drawn on a polar bond. It points toward the more electronegative atom (the δ− end) and its length represents how strong the pull is. Like any vector, two dipoles can cancel each other out if they are equal in magnitude and point in exactly opposite directions — leaving no net charge separation. If they don't fully cancel, the molecule has a net dipole and is polar.
What a dipole arrow represents

When two atoms share electrons unequally, the more electronegative atom pulls the electron density toward itself — creating a partial negative charge (δ−) on that end and a partial positive charge (δ+) on the other.

We show this as an arrow: tail at δ+, head at δ−. The arrow has two properties that matter:

  • Direction — which atom is pulling harder
  • Magnitude — how hard it is pulling (proportional to ΔEN; larger ΔEN = longer, more significant arrow)
H–F: electrons pulled strongly toward F. Arrow points H → F (δ+ side = crosshair tail).
Worked Example — Nonpolar molecule CO₂ Linear · 180°
1Both C=O bonds are polar (ΔEN = 1.0). Each has a dipole arrow pointing from C toward O.
2CO₂ is linear — the two arrows point in exactly opposite directions along the same axis.
3Equal magnitude + opposite direction = the vectors cancel completely. Net dipole = 0. CO₂ is nonpolar despite having polar bonds.
Worked Example — Polar molecule H₂O Bent · ~104.5°
1Both O–H bonds are polar (ΔEN = 1.4). Each has a dipole arrow pointing from H toward O.
2H₂O is bent — O has two lone pairs (not shown on model kit diagram) that push the bonds together. The arrows are not pointing in exactly opposite directions.
3The arrows partially cancel in the horizontal direction, but both have an upward component — they add together pointing toward O. Net dipole ≠ 0. H₂O is polar.
